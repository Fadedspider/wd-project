History

The genesis of the National Archives of India can be traced back to 1860 when Sandeman, the Civil Auditor, suggested the establishment of a 'Grand Central Archive' to manage the congestion of documents. This idea took shape in 1889 when Professor G.W. Forrest was appointed to examine Foreign Department records. His work led to the creation of the Imperial Records Department (IRD) in 1891 in Calcutta, with Forrest as its first head. The IRD's primary tasks included cataloguing records and establishing a central library.

With the capital moving to New Delhi in 1911, the IRD relocated in 1926. Post-independence, it was renamed the National Archives of India, with new leadership under Dr. S.N. Sen, who opened up records for research and established a Conservation Research Laboratory in 1940. His tenure saw significant developments in archival practices until 1949.

Since then, the National Archives has expanded its scope, playing a pivotal role in preserving India's cultural and historical records, with branches in Bhopal, Jaipur, Puducherry, and Bhubaneswar.

Preservation

The National Archives employs multiple strategies for document preservation, including preventive, curative, and restorative methods. The Conservation Research Laboratory, set up in 1941, has been instrumental in developing local restoration techniques and testing preservation materials. Notable innovations include the Solvent or Hand Lamination process and methods to restore ancient palm leaves.

Their microfilming program, ongoing for over three decades, is crucial for preventing document degradation due to use or aging. The Reprography Division ensures both scholar access and preservation through microfilming, with digital conversions for modern archival management systems.
